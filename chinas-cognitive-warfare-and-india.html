China's Cognitive Warfare and India

Srikanth Kondapalli

A strategic reading of China?s cognitive warfare toolkit, its use against multiple adversaries, and the implications for India?s institutions, media ecosystem, and national resilience.

1. Over the past few decades, nations have increasingly employed forms of warfare beyond traditional methods. With the two Gulf Wars in 1991 and 2003 and the recent Azerbaijan-Armenian drone warfare, Russia-Ukraine multi-domain warfare and Isreal-Gaza-Iran precision and information warfare, an array of tools were employed to fulfil objectives of the state. Increasingly, in the above wars and with more research, nations are resorting to non-kinetic means as kinetic warfare has become costly and its success unpredictable. One of these warfare methods is in the cognitive domain that attempts to influence the adversary’s perception by targeted disinformation campaigns, will to fight, morale, altering strategic narratives and influence the media and the decision-making processes of the opposing leadership. In 2003, China adopted “three warfares” (in legal, media and psychological domains) and it refined this to include cognitive warfare over a period of time since 2014. China had included cognitive war in the PLA’s “intelligentized” warfare with AI, cyber tools, data analytics for real time control and others. Following ancient Chinese strategician Sun Zi, China intends to “win without fighting” by influencing the opponent’s leadership, armed forces and the society in China’s favour. Cognitive warfare also prepares the groundwork for any eventual kinetic conflict with a sapped adversary.

2. China’s instruments in waging cognitive warfare includes flexible and diversified civil-military apparatuses – all under the communist party’s leadership - like United Front Work Department, Ministry of State Security, PLA General Staff Department units, PLA’s Information Support Force, PLA Cyberspace Force, five theatre commands and their political work departments against specific adversary, PLA numbered units such as 61398 and 69010, Cyberspace Administration of China, Propaganda Department of the communist party, the party-state controlled media outlets and others. China’s politburo standing committee level small leading groups, National Security Commission, Foreign Affairs Commission and others provide guidance for these units. While protecting its own cyber space with a “Great Wall” of blocking all sites, China floods the foreign media with bots and deepfakes to paint a picture of a peacefully rising China that is single-mindedly concerned about the well-being of the global population, while denigrating adversaries.

Cognitive Warfare on Adversaries

3. China has many adversaries due to its territorial ambitions and aggressive postures to unresolved disputes. China’s literature identifies Taiwan as a primary challenge and as an unfinished civil war of the 1940s when the communist party forced the Kuomintang to shift to Taiwan. Military skirmishes continued for decades without clearly identifying a winner as the United States began to support Taiwan. China had attempted all methods in its inventory – military buildup, ADIZ incursions, crossing the Taiwan Straits median line, psychological attrition, strategic competition, economic and diplomatic enticement or coercion or political offers - to subjugate Taiwan. None of these worked for China which in the last three years had escalated to an almost daily military exercises close to Taiwan’s 12 nautical mile limits. China knows that a kinetic war here is costly and unpredictable and may even cost China its rise and the communist party its legitimacy to rule. One alternative is cognitive war. Today, China employs a variety of such warfare on Taiwan – including targeting public opinion towards reunification, legal and psychological campaigns, exploiting Taiwan’s democratic system’s transparency and media and erode public support, break political coalitions, utilising mainland’s spouses, questioning the US/Japanese commitments and others. In addition to the PLA’s Eastern Theatre Command, China’s Taiwan Affairs Office and others have used cognitive warfare against Taiwan. It had also launched massive cognitive campaign in Taiwan elections last year but in vain. China invites scores of students for studies, as well as leaders of political parties and actively influences the decision-making processes in Taiwan.

4. A second major adversary for China is Japan on the Senkaku islands issue and in general to reverse the narrative of China’s defeat in 1894. Japan, which ruled over Taiwan from 1895 to 1945, also has an interest in Taiwan’s stability and prosperity. Even though Japan contributed to the current rise of China through official development assistance since 1972 and investments and technology transfers, Beijing is conductive intensive cognitive warfare on Japan’s military alliance with the US, historical issues and recently escalated on Japan’s Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s “existential threat” comments.

5. South China Sea disputed islands with the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia and Brunei have triggered intensive cognitive warfare operations by China. Even though a July 2016 ruling by the Permanent Court of Arbitration dismissed any “historical claims” in the region, or drawing EEZs from artificial reefs, China has intensified its claims to the region though divide and rule of the Southeast Asian countries, offering them upgrades to the free trade area and most significantly influence operations to sow discord and confusion among the Filipinos and others and their resolve. Specifically, on the Philippines, the tactics include propaganda campaign that these are “Chinese waters” from time immemorial and the media personnel of Philippines were shoed away from covering these areas or others subjected to water cannons.

India

6. China has been directing its cognitive warfare on India for some years now. Like India, China has a large population and in July 2022, India overtook China as the largest populated country. Some Chinese commentators began airing views that India will not be able to mange such a large population, their education and social welfare needs. Secondly, India is the 4th largest economy in the world while China is the 2nd largest. Several Chinese media claimed that India’s poverty alleviation, economic growth, manufacturing sector and others are weak, even though it is only recently that China claimed to have overcome “absolute poverty”, while its economic growth rates have halved. China’s income inequalities is also one of the highest in the world. Thirdly, India’s strategic depth and its conventional and strategic deterrence capabilities are a major concern for China and the Galwan border clashes led to a stalemate for four years and China’s companies are estimated to have lost nearly $150 billion in the Indian market. Fourthly, China’s rising nationalism is matched by the rise of nationalism in India and the strong leadership that emerged since 2014, breaking nearly three decades of stultifying coalition governments. Fifthly, continuous democratic experiments in India and its competitive politics are in sharp contrast to the authoritarian communist party leadership in China. This is an ideological problem emerging between India and China that is now being addressed by China in applying cognitive warfare techniques of denigrating India’s democracy and exporting China’s authoritarian model. Following this, China keeps in close touch with the opposition parties in India and tries to convince that resistance to territorial aggression or China’s economic model implantation is futile or costly for India. Through public outreach, China intends to control the narrative and deplete the public trust in the government and strategic clarity. Sixthly, China is viewing with concern the rise of a robust middle class in India which is more than 400 million people. With India banning TikTok, UC Browser, Byte Dance and other over 200 apps, banning Chinese investments in sensitive areas as well as shutting Confucious centres, restricting visas and direct flights, its influence operations will have limited appeal in India.

7. Nevertheless, China continues to target India with cognitive warfare. During the Galwan border clashes with India (that coincided with the spread of Covid pandemic from China), China had enhanced its cognitive warfare. China stated that Galwan represents its sovereignty and that “Galwan always belonged to China” without showing any documentary evidence, even though all along it mentioned the western sector as a disputed territory. It also criticised the Indian Parliament resolution of 2019 in creation of Ladakh Union Territory as “illegal”. While China developed border infrastructure in Tibet and Xinjiang since the 1980s, it criticised Indian building of roads such as the Daulat Beg Oldie -Darbuk -Shyok road. China also stated that “the right and wrong of what happened at Galwan is known to everyone” without elaborating what is right and wrong in this incident. It even argued that China’s position in the clashes is panted as “just” even though 20 Indian soldiers were killed in a barbaric manner. Several communist party-controlled media outlets warned India in developing relations with the United States and others, even though it has developed such relations with the US since 1971. Some waged a psychological war by suggesting that India should remember and learn from the 1962 set-back. Also, while China insists that India follow “one China” policy, Beijing hardly cares for “one India” policy on Kashmir, Khalistan movement or insurgencies in northeast. On the contrary, China had tried to sow seeds of discord on these issues. On Arunachal Pradesh, China’s foreign ministry and its spokespersons weave a controlled narrative with periodic diplomatic messaging of firmness and unrelenting strategic competition with India. During the Doklam crisis in Bhutan, China’s media released a video lampooning the English accent of Indian soldiers.

8. China’s cognitive warfare reached its peak during the Covid pandemic. China penalised countries which questioned China’s handling of the spread of the virus from Wuhan, shut down any demand for objective investigation into the issue, released a white paper praising itself for being “effective and transparent” and criticised democratic countries for the spread of havoc, including in India. While the virus outbreak is not the handiwork of India, on the other hand, India launched “vaccine maitri” of donating vaccines to the needy Global South countries. One of the most bizarre of these warfares is to depict, by the party-state media, of the funeral pyres in India to China’s launch of satellites.

9. It was during the Operation Sindoor, that cognitive warfare of China reached its cresendo against India. China’s party-state controlled media outlets like Guancha and others dished out millions of video clippings – thus saturating cyber space with curated narratives - about alleged J-10 fighter aircraft of China firing PL series of air-to-air missile that is said to have downed Indian Rafale aircraft. China also provided satellite information and C4ISR support to Pakistan on Indian positions. This is intended to degrade Indian command confidence, create doubts and distrust in the Indian armed forces and increase the sale of its military equipment abroad.

10. India had rejected several of China’s grand initiatives like Belt and Road Initiative (that violated India’s sovereignty), Community of Shared Destiny, Global Security Initiative, Global Developmental Initiative, Global Civilisational Initiative and the recent Global Governance Initiative. On the last, India suggests to rule of law while global governance has been weakened under the UN system. Such a rejection is unnerving for China given the Indian standing in the Global South countries. In order to address such recalcitrance, Beijing had resorted to cognitive warfare through its United Front Work Department and other agencies targeting Indians.

Conclusion

11. Short of an open and declared kinetic war, China’s cognitive warfare is waged in a “grey zone” and as such its successful applications are unknown. In some cases, such as in Taiwan, Philippines or India, many failures are actually discernible. However, specifically in the domains of influencing the opposing leadership’s perceptions and the will to fight, it creates conditions for fissures in the adversary’s leadership as well as success in a future potential kinetic war. China is also using new technologies to augment this warfare for more effectiveness. Thus, AI and brain machine interface developments are expected to further enhance the effectiveness of the cognitive warfare. China made major strides in this domain.

12. However, the above also raises issues on preparing and countering effectively China’s cognitive warfare. A few recommendations are as follows: Like Taiwan, India needs to establish a unified nodal cognitive warfare department to counter offensive narratives and “three warfares”. Cognitive warfare thrives when mind games are played on the adversary. India needs to build resilient narratives across the board on the achievements it made in a transparent manner. To secure cognitive and cyber domains, India needs to actively counter false narratives and lies through vibrant exercise of democratic debate in the mainstream and social media outlets. India needs to bridge the fissures within by following a “whole of the nation” approach. During the Galwan crisis and aftermath, India achieved for the first time a “whole of government” approach, even though many of its consumers imported Chinese-made goods. This needs to be corrected. Penetration of Chinese tech firms in India need to be monitored and countermeasures undertaken. India needs to diversify its economic ties and reduce economic vulnerabilities of depending on one single country and need to explore friends and partners in the international system. India’s signing of free trade agreements with UK, Oman, New Zealand and others is in this direction.
